Marketing AI

How I use AI in marketing: from dashboard interaction to fresher reporting and faster testing.

I use AI where it shortens the distance between question and action. That includes asking follow-up questions of dashboards, extracting and reshaping messy data, cleaning and matching records, generating test ideas, prioritizing growth work, supporting statistical modeling, and pairing Python with automations so reporting stays as fresh as the source systems allow.

Working rules

  • Use AI on top of a trusted metric layer, not instead of one.
  • Keep a human in the loop for spend, revenue, and funnel decisions.
  • Use Python and automation when freshness matters more than slide polish.
  • Let the best AI answers graduate into durable scorecards and workflows.

Where AI Fits

The marketing uses that are actually worth doing.

The best fit is not â€œAI everywhere.â€ It is using AI in the places where marketing teams lose time, miss signal, or wait too long for an answer. These are the main lanes I would use it in today.

Dashboard interaction

Use AI to answer the follow-up question dashboards usually miss: what changed, why it changed, and what needs attention next.

Data extraction

Pull usable fields from exports, PDFs, spreadsheets, call logs, notes, and APIs without hand-cleaning every source from scratch.

Data manipulation

Reshape, match, group, and standardize raw fields into reporting-ready tables that support scorecards, briefs, and models.

Cleaning and QA

Flag duplicates, taxonomy drift, missing source tags, malformed dates, and suspicious outliers before they distort reporting.

Growth testing and ideation

Generate hypotheses, summarize prior learnings, propose segments or offers, and draft test plans that the team can refine.

Prioritization

Rank the backlog by likely impact, confidence, effort, and data readiness so the team is not guessing what to work on first.

Statistical modeling

Support simple forecasting, seasonality analysis, response curves, anomaly detection, and scenario planning in plain language.

Agents and automation

Use scheduled jobs, AI agents, and Python refresh loops to retrieve fresh data, validate it, and push summaries or alerts on a useful cadence.

Recommended additions

Voice-of-customer summaries, call transcript clustering, weekly executive briefs, and martech taxonomy audits are all strong low-friction uses.

Dashboard Interaction

Dashboards answer the first question. AI is useful when the next question shows up.

I like AI layered on top of a stable scorecard so it can explain movement, cut results by segment, compare periods, and answer plain-language questions without turning every follow-up into a new dashboard build.

Natural-language analysis

Ask which channels drove booked jobs, which campaigns lost efficiency, or where cost per booked customer is rising.

Disposable visuals

Generate a one-off chart, table, or variance breakdown for the question at hand, then promote it to a permanent scorecard only if it proves useful.

Executive summaries

Turn a dense reporting view into a plain-language brief: what moved, why it matters, and what the team should do next.

Data Workflows

AI is just as useful before the dashboard as it is on top of it.

Some of the best wins come from extraction, manipulation, and cleaning work. AI can help turn messy source data into something reporting can trust, especially when paired with Python for repeatable retrieval and transformation.

Step 1

Pull

Retrieve source data from APIs, exports, spreadsheets, call systems, or PDFs.

Step 2

Normalize

Standardize date formats, field names, campaign names, source labels, and stage definitions.

Step 3

Match

Join spend, lead, call, booking, and revenue records into one operational view.

Step 4

Validate

Catch duplicates, nulls, taxonomy drift, and suspicious anomalies before publishing.

Step 5

Publish

Refresh dashboards, scorecards, or briefs on the cadence the team actually needs.

Growth Testing

AI is strong when the team needs more test ideas and faster readouts.

Once the measurement layer is stable, AI can help the team move faster by drafting hypotheses, surfacing segment opportunities, clustering prior wins and losses, and summarizing test outcomes in a way leaders can scan quickly.

Hypothesis generation

Draft ideas around channels, offers, landing pages, follow-up timing, and audience segments without waiting for a long planning cycle.

Learning synthesis

Summarize what the last few tests suggest so the next experiment is informed by patterns instead of memory.

Faster readouts

Turn a test result into a short decision memo: scale it, revise it, or stop it.

Prioritization

One of the most useful AI jobs is helping sort the backlog.

Marketing teams do not usually suffer from a lack of ideas. They suffer from too many ideas competing at once. AI can help rank work by likely impact, confidence, effort, and data readiness so the team focuses on what matters most.

Impact

Estimate which ideas are most likely to move booked customers, ROAS, or funnel velocity.

Confidence

Look for support from prior tests, segment behavior, historical seasonality, or repeated friction patterns.

Effort and readiness

Account for dependencies, creative effort, implementation time, and whether the measurement layer is ready to judge the result.

Modeling

AI is useful in modeling when it supports judgment instead of pretending to replace it.

I think the strongest use is pragmatic: forecasting booked volume, mapping seasonality, spotting anomalies, comparing scenarios, and helping explain model outputs in plain language. It should support decisions, not become a black-box flex.

Forecasting

Estimate likely booked customers, calls, or revenue by week so budget and staffing decisions are less reactive.

Seasonality and response curves

Use prior movement to understand when demand normally rises, falls, or saturates.

Explanation layer

Translate the model into simple business language: what the scenario means, what changed, and what to watch next.

Guardrails

The value of AI depends on the discipline around it.

AI becomes useful when the team is clear about definitions, data confidence, privacy, and where human review still matters. These are the rules I would want in place around any marketing AI workflow.

Trusted source first

AI should sit on top of source systems and documented definitions, not invent the metric layer from scratch.

Human review on spend decisions

Budget moves, attribution interpretation, and revenue claims still need human judgment and context.

Document the logic

Prompting, transformation rules, metric definitions, and automation steps should be easy to inspect and update.

Keep confidence visible

If match rates are weak or a source system is delayed, the AI output should surface that instead of hiding it.

Use the right cadence

Not every workflow needs to be instant. Fresh enough is usually the better target than performative real-time.

Promote the durable answers

When the same AI question keeps coming back, it should graduate into a stable scorecard, monitor, or recurring brief.
